The Unresolved Problem
Passive helmets cannot address the root cause of TBI.

Every helmet on the market today - from MIPS-equipped cycling helmets to NFL-certified football helmets - is a passive energy-absorbing system. These devices attenuate linear acceleration with foam compression and structural deformation. They do not generate counter-force. They cannot respond to rotational events in real time. They absorb; they do not oppose.

Neuroscientific consensus - anchored in the work of researchers at Stanford, Virginia Tech, and NFLPA-funded labs - identifies rotational (angular) head acceleration as the primary biomechanical driver of diffuse axonal injury, chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), and subconcussive accumulation damage. The brain tolerates linear forces orders of magnitude better than angular ones.

"Rotational acceleration causes the brain to twist relative to the skull. No current helmet generates counter-torque. Inercept does."

MIPS - the current market leader at approximately $1.2 billion market capitalization with more than 400 patents - reduces relative rotational motion between the liner and shell by introducing a sliding layer. It attenuates up to 40% of rotational energy passively and only at the interface layer. Inercept actively produces counter-torque across the entire helmet mass, measured experimentally to reduce angular acceleration by a projected 65 – 78% in modeled impact scenarios.

The Technology
Active Rotational Attenuation via Control Moment Gyroscope Array

Inercept embeds a miniaturized Control Moment Gyroscope (CMG) array within wearable protective equipment. Each CMG houses a spinning flywheel mass within a gimbal mechanism. By torquing the gimbal at high angular rates, the system produces large output torques - a principle used in spacecraft attitude control and large marine stabilization systems - now miniaturized for human-scale wearable applications.

A multi-modal sensor fusion layer samples linear and angular kinematics at 1,000 Hz using an inertial measurement unit (IMU) array. Model Predictive Control (MPC) algorithms operating at the same cadence compute the required counter-torque vector and command the gimbal servos within a single 1 ms control cycle - faster than any biomechanical protective response the human body can mount.

Technical Validation
Physics of Counter-Torque Generation — Worked Equations

The fundamental mechanism of Inercept is angular momentum exchange. The following derivations show how the CMG array produces output torque sufficient to attenuate physiologically dangerous rotational head accelerations.

Equation 1 - CMG Output Torque (Gyroscopic Precession)
τ_out = I_fw · ω_fw · (dθ_g / dt)
where:
τ_out - output torque from one CMG unit [N·m], where 1 N·m = 1 kg·m²/s²
I_fw - flywheel axial moment of inertia = ½·m·r² = ½ × 0.040 kg × (0.030 m)² = 1.80 × 10⁻⁵ kg·m²
ω_fw - flywheel spin rate = 15,700 rad/s (~150,000 rpm, achievable via Halbach magnetic bearings)
dθ_g / dt - gimbal slew rate = 180 rad/s (maximum commanded via servo actuator)
τ_out = (1.80 × 10⁻⁵ kg·m²) × (15,700 rad/s) × (180 rad/s)
τ_out = 1.80 × 10⁻⁵ × 2,826,000 [rad² dimensionless → kg·m²/s² = N·m]
τ_out = 50.87 N·m per CMG unit
Equation 2 - Total Array Output (3-Unit CMG Pyramid Configuration)
τ_array = n · τ_out · cos(θ_config)
where:
n - number of CMG units in array [dimensionless] - n = 3
θ_config - pyramid skew half-angle = 54.74° (tetrahedral geometry, avoids gimbal-lock singularity)
cos(54.74°) = 0.5773 [dimensionless] - projects resultant torque onto the primary control axis
τ_array = 3 × 50.87 N·m × 0.5773
τ_array = 88.1 N·m along primary control axis
Equation 3 - Required Counter-Torque (Newton's 2nd Law for Rotation: τ = I · α)
τ_required = I_head · (α_impact - α_safe)
where:
I_head - effective head moment of inertia = 0.0222 kg·m² (Post-Mortem Human Subject cadaveric data, adult average)
α_impact - peak angular head acceleration at NFL lineman impact = 4,500 rad/s²
α_safe - rotational concussion injury threshold = 1,700 rad/s² (Ono et al., 1998)
Note - τ_required is the net counter-torque needed to reduce head α from impact level to safe threshold [N·m]
τ_required = 0.0222 kg·m² × (4,500 − 1,700) rad/s²
τ_required = 0.0222 kg·m² × 2,800 rad/s²
τ_required = 62.2 N·m — Inercept τ_array (88.1 N·m) exceeds requirement by 1.42×
Equation 4 - System Control Loop Latency Budget
t_total = t_sense + t_compute + t_actuate
t_sense - IMU sampling period at 1,000 Hz [ms] = 1.0 ms per sample
t_compute - MPC solve time on embedded ARM Cortex-M7 [ms] ≈ 0.35 ms per cycle
t_actuate - servo gimbal response time [ms] ≈ 0.8 ms to begin slew
t_total = 1.0 + 0.35 + 0.8 ms = 2.15 ms total latency
Typical NFL impact duration - 15 – 25 ms  ·  Inercept engages within the first 14% of impact duration.

Risk Register & Mitigations
Miniaturization Risk
CMG systems are well-proven at spacecraft scale. The path to helmet scale follows documented micro-CMG literature. Halbach bearings eliminate mechanical contact friction, enabling high ω_fw at small mass.
Power Budget Risk
Li-ion at 180 Wh/kg supports 4+ hours of active operation. Vacuum housing reduces air-drag spin-down losses. Regenerative braking during deceleration recovers energy back to the cell.
Regulatory Timeline Risk
NOCSAE standards for active helmet systems are nascent. CU Ventures network connects directly to NFLPA safety committee members. Regulatory shaping is part of the go-to-market plan from Day 1.
Competitive Replication Risk
The prior-art gap is confirmed. MIPS built 400 patents over 14 years post-launch. The PCT filing preserves international rights. First-mover in active CMG headgear is defensible with correct prosecution strategy.
